## The Origin — Why This App Exists

This application was not designed from a market analysis. It was designed from experience.

Ryan received a new custom-built computer in June 2026. Within the first hours, it was crashing — BSOD errors, system shutdowns, instability. He opened Claude Code and walked through the entire diagnostic process across two sessions: isolating the bad RAM, repositioning sticks, running SFC and DISM, correcting Windows Update, checking GPU and CPU temperatures, network speed, drive health, virus scan — every layer of the machine, methodically.

It worked. The machine was fixed. And after it was fixed, Ryan said: "Man, that is a useful tool. There would have been no way for me to be able to troubleshoot my own computer in the way that you did it."

The diagnostic process we ran on his machine — keystroke by keystroke, screenshot by screenshot, step by step at the exact level the person in front of the screen needed — became the blueprint for Amit Computer Value.

What Ryan experienced firsthand:
- If the builder had run a certification before delivery, the bad RAM would have been caught at the bench — not on Ryan's time
- The difference between someone who knows how to run diagnostics and someone who doesn't is enormous — and it is the gap Amit fills
- The app knows what every possible output looks like. When a command runs and the screen shows something unexpected, Amit doesn't leave the person guessing. The app already knows what that output means and says so: "You may see this message. It simply means the service was already off. This is fine. Continue."

That last piece — the app knowing every possible outcome — is what makes this a companion and not a tutorial. A tutorial shows you what to do when things go right. Amit shows you what to do when they don't.

## Market Valuation — Range Model

The valuation is the most credibility-sensitive part of the system. A single number that doesn't match reality breaks trust across the entire Amit system — not just this app.

The correct model: a **range**, not a single number.

Built from two inputs:
1. **Depreciation curve** — computers lose value on a known curve from the date of manufacture. Component age factors into the baseline range.
2. **Current market data** — *sold* prices, not listed prices. eBay sold listings, Newegg, current retail for new equivalents. Listed prices are what people ask. Sold prices are what machines actually trade for.

The scan result then positions the machine within that range.

Output example: *"Based on current market data and component age, fair market value for this configuration is $650–$750. Your machine's health score of 91% puts it at the higher end of that range."*

This is honest about uncertainty while still being useful. It shows its work. A buyer can trust it because the reasoning is visible.

## The Scoring System

Two scores. Both permanent. Both public.

**Score 1 — The Machine:** This specific computer, tested this day, valued this day. Health rating in percentage of capacity from new. Component breakdown. Timestamped. Travels with the machine.

**Score 2 — The Builder:** Cumulative record across every certified build. Pass rate. Configuration miss rate. Buyer ratings. Public to every buyer who scans the QR code.

Build classifications have their own baselines:
- Manufacturer Level (factory new) — highest standard
- Custom New (all new components, custom assembled) — high standard
- Hybrid (mix of new and used) — adjusted standard
- Custom Rebuilt (all used) — used component standard

A hybrid scoring 94% on hybrid standards is an honest 94%. Never compared to the wrong baseline.

## AUDIT FINDINGS — 2026-06-08 (Session 17)

Two gaps that must be resolved before Stage 1 build begins:

- **Payment mechanism for Stage 1 is unresolved.** The spec says "$5 per report" on a self-contained HTML file. A static HTML file cannot process payments on its own. Stripe, PayPal, or another integration is required. Without this decision, Stage 1 is a free tool, not a revenue engine. Decide the payment mechanism before writing the first line of Stage 1 HTML.
- **Remove "backed certification" from Stage 1.** The spec promises Amit will "stand behind" ratings above 95%. This creates legal liability with no legal framework to support it. What does "Amit stands behind it" mean if a buyer relies on it and the machine fails? This belongs in a later stage after legal structure is in place. Remove from Stage 1 scope entirely.

## Key Design Principles

**The app knows every possible outcome.** For every command, every diagnostic step, every result — the app has already accounted for what success looks like, what failure looks like, what "this is normal" looks like, and what "stop and do this instead" looks like. The person is never left guessing.

**Level is selected at launch — then read continuously.** User declares beginner / intermediate / expert at the start. The app also reads the pattern of the exchange throughout and adjusts. A person can carry a sophisticated question borrowed from a forum post without actually understanding it. Amit reads the pattern, not just the declaration.

**The encouraging voice is never optional.** Even at expert level, the tone is Amit's tone. Patient. Direct. Never condescending. When a command fails — Amit does not report failure. Amit says: "This happened. Here is what it means. Here is the next step." That voice is the same voice in who_is_god.html. Same companion. Different domain.

**Build before reading results.** Complete the full diagnostic pass first, then evaluate and produce the report. Do not interrupt the flow after each individual step to deliver verdicts. Amit gathers everything, then speaks.

## The B2B Opportunity (Hold in Reserve)

The individual buyer/seller market is the entry point. The larger opportunity may be businesses.

- A small business buying 10 computers needs all 10 certified
- A repair shop certifying every machine they sell needs volume pricing
- An MSP (Managed Service Provider) managing client networks needs fleet reporting
- IT procurement teams need a paper trail for every asset

Subscription pricing for volume builders. Bulk certification packages. Fleet health dashboards. This is a different product than the consumer tool — same engine, different interface and pricing model.

Do not build for this in Stage 1. Let Stage 1 prove the concept with individual users. But hold the B2B angle in the architecture so it doesn't have to be reverse-engineered in later.
